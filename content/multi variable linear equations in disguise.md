#math/algebra , #math/algebra/inequality/equations , [[Linear Equation]], [[linear equations in disguise]]

Just like with normal [[Linear Equation\|Linear equations]], [[multi variable equations]] can also be in disguise.

## Problems
### 5.14, intro
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240429181546.png]]
- Our equations are:
		- $\sqrt a + \sqrt b =37$
		- $\sqrt a=2\sqrt b+10$
	- We can organize by putting all variables on one side
		- $\sqrt a+\sqrt b=37$
		- $\sqrt a-2\sqrt b=10$
	- We can use [[Elimination]] to cancel out the $a$ by subtracting both equations, so we are left with:
		- $3\sqrt b=27$
	- Dividing by $3$ gives 
		- $\sqrt b=9$
	- Substitute this in either equation and we find that
		- $\sqrt a=28$
	- The problem is not asking us for the values of $\sqrt a$ or $\sqrt b$, it's asking us for just $a$ and $b$, we do this by just squaring the equations we have now
		- $\sqrt b=9$, we [[Perfect Squares\|Square]] to get $b=81$
		- $\sqrt a=28$, we square to get $a=784$
- Additionally, we can also set $r=\sqrt a$ and $s=\sqrt b$ through [[substitution]]
	- Our system just becomes
		- $r+b=37$
		- $r=2s+10$
	- We can see that this is just a normal system of equations



### 5.15
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240429182832.png]]
- HmmMMMMMmmmMMMMMM, we have [[variable math\|variables]] in the [[Denominator]] eh? 
	- We can multiply the first equation by $2xy$ and the second by $xy$ to get rid of the fractions, we have:
		- $6y-4x=-7xy$
		- $6y+4x=9xy$
	- seeing those $xy$ terms might lead us to give up, but seeing that those $x$ terms will [[cancel out]] once we add the equations through [[Elimination]], it can't hurt to try right? [[Experimentation and Exploration are the key to solving problems, don't be afraid to take a step if you don't know what the next one will be]] our equation will be:
		- $12y=2xy$
	- Now we see that we can actually divide the whole equation by $y$, so lets do that
		- $12=2x$
	- We get $x$ by dividing by $2$
		- $x=6$
	- Plug in $x$ for any other equation and we get 
		- $y=\frac{1}{2}$
	- Our [[ordered pair]] is $\left( 6, \frac{1}{2} \right)$
- There is also an alternate way to do this
	- both equations contain the [[Reciprocal]] of $x$ and $y$, if we let $r=\frac{1}{x}$ and $s=\frac{1}{y}$ we get 
		- $3r-2s=-\frac{7}{2}$
		- $6r+4s=9$
	- Using elimination of substitution we find that $(r,s)$ is $\left( \frac{1}{6}, 2 \right)$, using our definitions of $r$ and $s$, we find that $(x,y)$ is $6, \frac{1}{2}$
