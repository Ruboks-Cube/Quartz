- When 2 variables are in Direct Proportion, it means the [[Ratio]] of the two (Or more) [[variable math\|variables]] is always the same we say the two values are in **Direct Proportion**. Direct Proportion means the quotient of the 2 **variables is always the same.**
	- We use Direct Proportion in our daily lives. sometimes you don't even realize you're using them

> Why is direct a simple function? Like a [[Linear Equation]]
> 
> The variables are *directly* related to each other. $\frac{y}{x}$ always equals $k$. 


# Problems
## Key Ideas
- If $x$ and $y$ are directly proportional, that means the [[Fraction\|Quotient]] of $\frac{x}{y}$ is constant. In other words, $\frac{x}{y}=k$ always. Another way to say this is that $x$ is a multiple of $y$ always, we have $x=ky$
- [[Direct Proportion]] problems are similar to normal [[Ratio]] problems, a <mark class="hltr-yellow">key step in solving them is to find the constant ratio of quantities</mark>. The Constant ratio of quantities is the <mark class="hltr-green">smallest group basically, a simplified version of the ratio</mark>, a [[Simplifying Fractions\|Simplified Fraction]]. In [[Direct Proportion#7.1\|Problem 7.1]], the <mark class="hltr-red">constant ratio of quantities</mark> for (a) was $9:5$. It can sometimes be referred to as Ratio of Proportionality, or constant of proportionality, and later, [[slope]].
- We can find the constant of proportionality to solve a **direct proportions** problem, or we can find how many [[Ratios come in groups\|Groups of the ratio]] there is by comparing like quantities like we did in solution 2 of [[Direct Proportion#7.2\|Problem 7.2]]. Both work, and both are important to understand.

- Solution
	- The weight of the person and how much mL is given is directly proportional, so we have: $\frac{lb}{ml}=\frac{140}{100}$. [[Simplifying Fractions\|Simplifying the fraction]] gives $\frac{7}{5}$.
	- We let $x$ be the amount of medicine for Mr. Jones in mL. Mr. Jones weighs 120, so we have $\frac{120}{x}=\frac{7}{5}$. Solving this [[equations\|Equation]] gives $85 \frac{5}{7}mL$
	- We could have also solved this by noticing that Mr. Jones weighs $\frac{120}{140}$ or $\frac{6}{7}$ of the "model" patient, so he needs $\frac{6}{7}$ as much medicine. $\frac{6}{7}*100=85 \frac{5}{7}$. This goes along with solving the [[Direct Proportion]] through [[Ratios come in groups\|ratio groups]] since we notice that we multiply $\frac{6}{7}$ by $140$ to get $120$, to keep the ratio [[equivalent]]/[[Proportion\|Proportional]] we multiply 100 by $\frac{6}{7}$ as well.
