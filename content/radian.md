# Alternate explanation
![[Files/Untitled 5.png| Diagram]]
- We have this circle, how many [[diameter\|Diameters]] is the [[circumference]]?
	- That’s [[pi]] diameters
	- How many [[radius\|radii]] would make the [[circumference]]? That would be $2\pi r$
		- [[radius]] is half the [[diameter]]
	- So what would $\pi r$ be?
	- That would be half the circumference
	- $\pi$ RADII is half the circumference
- How do we use this to measure angles?
- If we had a point at the center of a circle $O$, and made an angle using that point, we could measure the amount of [[radius\|radii]] of that [[arc]] that [[subtend]] the [[Angles\|angle]]
	- $\pi$ radians is $180^{\circ}$ because if we make a circle around a point and create a straight line, the [[arc]] subtending that would have a length of pi radii

- Lets say we construct a [[Circle]] with [[radius]] $r$ and construct an angle $\theta$
	- Lets say this [[Angles\|angle]] of ours is the exact right angle so that the [[arc]] that [[subtend\|subtends]] this angle has a length of the [[radius]] 
		![[Files/Pasted image 20240805135811.png]]
	- if you wanted to make a new way to define angles, what would you say $\theta$ was with the information given? How many *radians* is $\theta$?
		- We would say that $\theta$ is $1$ radian cause the [[arc]] that [[subtend\|subtends]] $\theta$ has the [[arc length\|length]] of $r$ which is the same as it's [[radius]]. 
	- So, to calculate how many radians something is, we js do $\frac{\text{length of arc}}{\text{length of radius}}$ to see what fraction of the radius the [[arc]] is. 
	- ! We can rearrange $\theta(\text{Length of radius})=\frac{\text{length of arc}}{\text{length of radius}}$ -> 
		- $\theta=\frac{\text{Central angle}}{360^{\circ}}*2\pi r* \frac{1}{r}$ This simplifies to:
		- $\theta = \frac{\text{Central Angle}}{180^{\circ}}*\pi$ 
	- [[pi]] radians would be like 3.14 [[radius\|Radii]] [[subtend\|subtending]] an [[arc]].
	




- The radian measure of an [[Angles\|angle]] $\theta$ is defined as a [[Rate\|constant of proportionality]] given by the [[Ratio]] of the [[arc length]] to its [[radius]]:
- $\theta=\frac{\text{length of arc AB}}{r}$ 
- By rearranging the equation for [[arc length]] you can find the radian measure of a [[central angle]] using its measure in degrees
