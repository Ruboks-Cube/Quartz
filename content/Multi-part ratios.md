## First Example Problem
To get a specific shade of orange paint, we mix white red and yellow in the ratio of 2:5:4. How many gallons of each color do we need if we need 6 gallons of orange paint?

### algebraic solution

![[Files/Images/Multi-part ratios 2024-01-16 11.34.21.excalidraw\|500]]
By getting the [[Linear Equation]] 2x+5x+4x=6 by realizing that 2x of white paint (you can think of x as some amount of gallons) + 5x of red paint + 4x of yellow paint equals 6 gallons. By adding them and then dividing 6 by 11, we get the size of the containers, 6/11ths of a gallon.

[[addition]], [[Division]]



### Parts of a whole solution
![[Files/Images/Multi-part ratios 2024-01-17 18.35.16.excalidraw]]

White paint is 2:11, meaning the white paint is 2/11ths of the final mixture. Since the final mixture is 6 gallons, 2/11ths of 6 is `2/11*6=12/11`.

## Second Example Problem
There are cash prizes for the top 3 in a competition. The cash prize ratio of the top 3 is 5:3:2. If the first cash prize is 20,000 more than the 3rd cash prize, what is the second cash prize.

5x-2x=20,000

Here we find out what number times 5 and 2 will lead to them having a difference of 20,000. This will give us the [[Scale Factor]]
