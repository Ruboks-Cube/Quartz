#### An example of [[Combining Like terms]]
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240407164258.png]]
- (a) Michelle would have $5x$ Legos, Adam would have $3x$ Legos.
- (b) to simplify $5x+3x$ we can [[Factoring\|factor out]] the "x" to get $(5+3)x=8x$, this is known as [[Combining Like terms]]

#### Packs of gum
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240408194445.png]]
- (a) the expression would be $4x+5$

- (b) Nathan would have $7x+3$ pieces of gum

- (c) The total number of pieces would be $4x+5+7x+3$

- (d) We can simplify this by using the [[Commutative and Associative Property]] to get $4x+5+7x+3$, then through [[Combining Like terms]] we get $11x+8$

- (e) we simplify $(3r-2)+(3r-4)+(7-5r)$ by getting rid of the parenthesis to get $3r-2+3r-4+7-5r$, then we can simplify further by combining like terms. $r+1$.

#### Expressions with [[Exponents]]
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240408201538.png]]
- (a) using our [[Same Base exponent rules]] we can turn $y*y*y$ into $3y$
- (b) again, using our [[Same Base exponent rules]] we turn $y^2*y^4=y^6$
- (c) we turn $(2x)(3x^3)$ into $2*x*3*x^3$ so we have $6x^4$ [[Commutative and Associative Property]]
- (d) $(v^3)^5$ is a situation that is  [[Power to a power exponent rules]], where we have $v^3$ multiplied to itself 5 times, so we have $v^{15}$
- (e) $(2x)^3$ means we have 3 of $(2x)$, so we multiply the exponent both terms in $2x$ by 3 to get $2^3x^3$ which is $8x^3$
- Notice how these look a lot like the [[Distributive Property]]. It looks like we are distributing the 3.

> [!warning] $(2x)^3$ does not act the same way as $(2+x)^3$
> One is a [[Power of a sum]], while the other is a [[Product of Powers, quotient of powers\|Power of product]]

#### [[Distributive Property]] with [[variable math\|variables]]
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240413103014.png]]
There is nothing special about variables, we use the distributive property normally.

- (a) $2(x+7)=2x+14$
- (b) $2(x^2+3)+3(x^2-9)=2x^2+6+3x^2-27$
	- [[Combining Like terms\|Combine like terms]] to get $5x^2-21$
##### using distributive property to subtract
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240413103533.png]]
- (a) we could convert the feet into inches and then subtract, but we could use the distributive property to subtract it faster
	- $(7f+7i)-(5f+3i)$ is the expression that shows this situation, where f is feet and i is inches.
	- We distribute the $-1$ to get $7f+7i-5f-3i$ and combine like terms to get $2f+4i$
	- 2 feet 4 inches is our answer
- (b) we can simplify $(x+3)-(3x+9)$ by distributing the $-1$   [[distrubuting by -1]]
	- $(x+3)-3x-9$ and combine like terms to get $-2x-6$. 
- (b) $(t^2-5t+4)-(t^2-7)$ we get $(t^2-5t+4)-t^2+7$, remove the parenthesis, combine like terms, we get $-5t+11$ 

#### [[Factoring]] with [[variable math\|variables]]
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240413105441.png]]
- (a) $3(x+6)$, "divide" the 3 by the whole expression
- (b) $5(-3a^2+7)$, "divide" 5 by the whole expression since 15 and 35 have a factor of 5 in common
- (c) $x(3x+2)$ "divide" x by the whole expression, be careful since $x^2=x*x$ 
- (d) $2a(a^2+8a-4)$ "divide" 2a by the whole expression

We can also factor out whole entire expressions:
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240413110001.png]]
$2n+1$ is something the terms have in common, so lets factor that out
$(2n+1)(n+5)$, this actually leads us to a [[double distribution]] case, since we just factored out a whole expression containing 2 terms.

Another way to think about it is to [[substitution\|substitute]] the $2n+1$ with a single variable.

$nx+5x$ where $x=(2n+1)$. Now we can factor out the x to get ${} x(n+5)$. Plug the $2n+1$ back in and we get $(2n+1)(n+5)$.


> [!tip] substitution
> Substitution can often be used to simplify algebraic expressions/equations and are used often. It is very powerful 

##### Shauna's number trick
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240413110625.png]]
use $x$ as any even number.

$2(\frac{2x+48}{4}-7)-x$
We now have an expression to solve!

We [[Distributive Property\|Distribute]] the 2 to get $\frac{4x+96}{4}-14-x$, simplify $\frac{4x+96}{4}$ to get $x+24-14-x$ and finally [[Combining Like terms\|Combine like terms to get]] to get $10$. We now see that all the number trick did was make it so $x$ was eventually subtracted by itself to get just $10$.


#### [[Fraction\|Fractions]]
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240413111610.png]]

- (a)  $\frac{3\left(2x+3\right)}{12}=\frac{2x+3}{4}$, [[Simplifying Fractions]], [[Factoring]]

> [!warning] WARNING
> We CANNOT factor out a 2 from the numerator and denominator to simplify it since it's being added

- (b) $\frac{7t^{2}+3t}{t}$, we [[Factoring\|factor out]] the $t$ to get $\frac{t\left(7t+3\right)}{t}$, then end with $7t+3$
- (c) in this one we have both the numerator and the denominator to deal with $\frac{{3x-6}}{x^2-2x}$
	- factor out the $3$ from the numerator and an $x$ from the denominator to get $\frac{{3(x-2)}}{x(x-2)}$
	- The $(x-2)$'s [[cancel out]] leaving us with $\frac{3}{x}$

> [!tip] Quick Tip
> Factoring can help simplify fractions when both the numerator and denominator have variables


##### [[adding fractions with different denominators]] but with [[variable math\|variables]]

![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240414203105.png]]
- Multiply $3x*x$ to get $3x^2$. This is our common denominator. 
- We would have $\frac{2x}{3x^2}+\frac{9x}{3x^2}$ because of [[equivalent]] fractions, we multiply the numerator and denominator by the same thing
- Now add to get $\frac{11x}{3x^2}$ 
- We can factor an $x$ from the numerator and denominator to get $\frac{{x(11)}}{x(3x)}$
- 2 $x's$ are cancelled out to get $\frac{11}{3x}$


Now figure out how to do this with 2 terms in the denominator and you're set. (pssst, use the [[Distributive Property\|distributive property!]])
