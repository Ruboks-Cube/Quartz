#math/name , #math/algebra , [[algebra]]

- Relative Information in [[math]] is information that is presented in [[Ratio\|Ratios]] (how one value relates to another) so the actual values don't matter, we just know the values *relative* to the other value

> [!WARNING] WARNING
> - Only attempt to use relative information, or [[substitution]] when you know that changing the value will not affect the answer to a problem
> - If you aren't sure whether a value affects the answer to a problem, be safe and [[variables as unknown values\|Assign a variable]]

Here is an example of where we can use relative information to solve a problem:
### 6.19
- Key ideas
	- 
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240514193055.png]]
- Solution 1
	- To find the percentage of students who participate in MATHCOUNTS we must first find the ratio of the participants to non participants, we let $x$ represent the number of $7th$ graders
		- $1.5x+x$ represents the total amount of students, $1.5x$ representing the number of 8th graders
			- We take $6$% of $1.5x$ and $3$% of $x$ to get $0.09x+0.03x=0.12x$ which represents the NUMBER of students who participate in MATHCOUNTS
			- The ratio of this to the total amount of students is $0.12x:2.5x$, which is $\frac{0.12}{2.5}$
			- We have $\frac{0.12}{2.5}=\frac{x}{100}$, and this becomes a simple [[Percent Problems#What percent of x is y?\|What percent of x is y]] problem
		- This gives $x=0.048$ which is $4.8$%, so $4.8$% of 7th and 8th graders participate in MATHCOUNTS
	- Note that we did not figure out the exact number of students who went to the school, or were in 7th or 8th grade, in fact, this is impossible to do
		- We simply let $x$ become the amount of 7th graders, and hoped the $x$ would cancel out
		- The actual percentage will stay the same no matter what the $x$ is
		- Since the value of $x$ does not matter, we can "choose" or [[substitution\|Substitute a value for]] $x$, if we chose $100$, we would still get that $4.8$% of 8th and 7th graders went, a good way of checking our answer
	- This is a new concept called [[Relative Information]]
