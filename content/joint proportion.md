#math/algebra/inequality/equations/Ratios_Proportions_Rates , [[Proportion]]

- [[Direct Proportion]] and [[Inverse Proportion]] involve relationships between 2 [[variable math\|variables]]. Sometimes we have situations where more than 2 variables are related.


# problems
## Key Ideas
- Joint Proportion is when we have one relationship between 2 variables, and another with the same variable from the previous with another variable, and we find an [[Expression]] or [[equations\|Equation]] on how they are ALL related.

> [!WARNING] IMPORTANT
> We say a variable like $x$ is in jointly proportional to a group of other variables if $x$ is directly proportional to each of those variables. If all other variables are constant, like $x$ is jointly proportional to $y$ and $z$ we can write $x=kyz$ for where $k$ is a constant. We can write this as $\frac{x}{yz}=k$, showing that $x$ and $yz$ are directly proportional, and so is $\frac{x}{y}$ and $\frac{x}{z}$ to $y$ or $z$ on the other side.
> - JOINT PROPORTION IS WHEN ONE VARIABLE IS DIRECTLY PROPORTIONAL TO A PRODUCT OF OTHER VARIABLES
- A nice way to find joint proportions is to group the varying quantities ([[variable math\|variables]]) on one side of the equation so we're left with constants on the other, leaving us <mark class="hltr-yellow">a direct proportion.</mark>
- When we have joint proportion, like $\frac{x}{yz}$, $x$ is directly proportional to $yz$, and if the product of $yz$ gets bigger because like $y$ got doubled, or $z$ got doubled, then $x$ will increase by the same AMOUNT.
- If $x$ is [[Direct Proportion\|Directly Proportional]] to $y$, then we have $\frac{x}{y}=k$. If $x$ is in [[Direct Proportion]] to $z$, we have $\frac{x}{z}=k$. $x$ is directly proportional to both $y$ and $z$. If $z$ stays constant, we have $\frac{x}{y}=k*z$. If $y$ stays constant, we have $\frac{x}{z}=k*y$. (Ratio of $\frac{x}{z}*$ how many $y$'s are there when y is constant, think of $y$ and $z$ when they are constant as like [[Ratios come in groups\|Groupings]]). What if $z$ and $y$ are both **NOT** constant? We then have $\frac{x}{yz}=k$ (divide $y$ or $z$ by both sides). This makes it so $x$ is [[Direct Proportion\|Directly Proportional]] to the product of $yz$. If $y$ increases by some amount through multiplication, then $x$ also increases by the same factor, if $z$ increases by some amount, $x$ increases by the same [[Factor]]. 
- [[RT=D Rate times Time equals Distance]] is an example of Joint Proportion and occurs very frequently in problems.

- (a) if $T$, $R$, and $n$ are constant, this tells us that the product $PV$ is constant. This means that the pressure and volume of a gas are [[Inverse Proportion\|Inversely Proportional]] if $n$ and $T$ are constant. 
	- If temperature is constant, this means that pressure and volume must multiply to each other to get $nRT$. If Pressure increases, then Volume decreases and vice versa.
- (b) If $V$ is constant, the pressure and temperature of a gas are in [[Direct Proportion]] because we have $\frac{P}{T}=\frac{nr}{V}$.
	- Since $nr$ and $V$ are constant, the ratio of $P:T$ also remains constant. This shows that the pressure and temperature of a gas is [[Direct Proportion\|Directly Proportional]]. If the Volume is constant, then the ratio of pressure and temperature is always the same means more pressure = more temperature, less pressure = less temperature and vice versa.
- (c) Celsius and Fahrenheit allow for a value of $0$ and also [[Negative Number\|Negative Numbers]]. What would happen to the ideal gas law? $PV=nRT$. 
	- R remains constant, and Pressure, Volume, and the number of molecules cannot be negative. We are unable to measure the temperature on a scale that allows negative temperatures for the Ideal gas law. We must use an absolute measure of temperature. 
		- [[Kelvin Scale]]
- (d) As we did before, we put the changing quantities on the left, since those are the ones we need the relationship of. We have $\frac{PV}{T}=nR$. We know that $\frac{PV}{T}$ is constant, they are in [[Direct Proportion]]. 
	- $\frac{PV}{T}=nR$, this means that if $T$ gets bigger, $PV$ gets bigger, if $PV$ gets smaller, $T$ gets smaller. However, if $P$ gets bigger, then $V$ must get smaller unless $V$ gets bigger by the same factor, then $T$ would also get bigger by the same amount because of [[Direct Proportion]].
		- As the [[Multiplication\|Product]] of $PV$ gets bigger or smaller, $T$ gets bigger or smaller by the same factor. If $P$ increases by $50$%, we're multiplying $p$ by $\frac{3}{2}$, which means we multiply the whole product of $PV$ by $\frac{2}{3}$ because of basic [[Commutative and Associative Property]]. We have $\frac{\frac{3}{2}PV}{T}$. By increasing $P$ by $50$%, we increase the whole product of $PV$ by $50$%, so we also increase $T$ by $50$%, now we know what happens, lets actually solve the question.
	- If $P_{1}V_{1}T_{1}$ represent original values and $P_{2}V_{2}T_{2}$ represent the newer values we get: $\frac{P_{1}V_{1}}{T_{1}}=\frac{P_{2}V_{2}}{T_{2}}$ because the [[Ratio]] stays consistent since they are in [[Direct Proportion]]. Both $P_{1}V_{1}T_{1}$ and $P_{2}V_{2}T_{2}$ $=rn$. We have volume is tripled and pressure halved, so we have $\frac{\frac{p_{1}}{2}3v_{1}}{T_{2}}=\frac{P_{2}V_{2}}{T_{2}}$. Rearranging this gives $T_{2}=\frac{3}{2}T_{1}$ . 
 - We showed Temperature and Volume to be proportional when all else was constant, and Temperature and Pressure proportional when all else was constant as well. We say that Temperature is **Jointly Proportional** to pressure and Volume. Temperature was related to Pressure, and temperature was related to volume, so we made an equation to show how they are ALL related.
## 7.8
- Some joint proportion problems are joint proportion problems in disguise.
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240518121444.png]]
- Solution 1, my thinking:
	- 5 woodchucks can chuck 8 pieces of wood in 2 hours. $5:8:2$ is our [[Ratio]]. $5$ woodchucks, per 8 pieces, per 2 hours. 
	- We need to find an equation to represent this situation. If 5 woodchucks can chuck 8 pieces of wood in 2 hours, 10 woodchucks would be able to chuck 16 pieces of wood in 2 hours. 
	- We have $\frac{wood}{woodchucks}=1.6$ per 2 hours. Per hour would be $\frac{wood}{woodchucks}=0.8h$, where $h$ represents the number of hours. Dividing this equation by $h$ gives $\frac{wood}{woodchucks}* \frac{1}{h}=0.8$. This gives $\frac{wood}{woodchucks*h}=0.8$. This shows that the amount of wood pieces chucked is [[Direct Proportion\|Directly Proportional]] to $woodchucks*h$, woodchucks times hours. We have $1$ wood chuck, and $24$ hours, so we have $\frac{wood}{24}=0.8$. The amount of wood pieces chucked by 1 woodchuck in 24 hours would be $wood=19.2$ pieces.
- Solution 2, book way
	- If number of wood pieces doubles and the number of woodchucks stay the same, that means the number of time has doubled. Similarly, if the number of pieces of wood doubles and the time stays the same, then the amount of woodchucks double. Therefore, the amount of wood is [[Direct Proportion\|Directly Proportional]] to both the number of woodchucks and the amount of time needed. So, we must have $\frac{wood}{(woodchucks)(hours)}=k$, where $k$ is constant. 
		- Further explanation on how we got $\frac{wood}{(woodchucks)(hours)}=k$: 
			- TLDR: Wood is [[Direct Proportion\|Directly Proportional]] to $(woodchucks)(hours)$. If the product increases/decreases by some factor, then wood also decreases. Say we halve hours, $(woodchucks)\left( \frac{hours}{2} \right)$, due to the [[Commutative and Associative Property]], we have $(woodchucks)(hours)\left( \frac{1}{2} \right)$, so the whole product changes by $\frac{1}{2}$ and $x$ decreases by a [[Factor]] of $\frac{1}{2}$ as well! 
		- We wonder how much $1$ woodchuck can do in $24$ hours, and our original product was $10$. $\frac{24}{10}$ gives $2.4$, which is what we multiplied $woodchucks(hours)$ by to get $24$ as our new product. Multiplying $8$ by $2.4$ gives $19.2$
		- An algebraic way to do this would be to start with our original set $\frac{8}{(5)(2)}$ and take our new one $\frac{x}{(1)(24)}$ and set them equal to each other $\frac{8}{(5)(2)}=\frac{x}{(1)(24)}$. This works because the wood is in the [[Direct Proportion]] with the product of the denominator, solving for $x$ gives $19.2$

## 7.9 
- One Last EXAMPLE 
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240519102246.png]]
- Solution 1, my thinking
	- Dale drove $100$ miles at a constant [[Rate]] for $t$ time and $s$ speed.
	- The distance he travels is [[Direct Proportion\|Directly Proportional]] to ${} ts {}$, doubling his time gives us double the distance, double the speed gives us double the distance as well!
	- So we have $d$ ($d$ for distance) $\frac{d}{ts}=k$, where $k$ is the constant of proportionality.
	- Our original equation would be $\frac{100}{ts}=k$. Our next equation would be $\frac{d_{2}}{(3t)(2s)}=k$ because we are tripling the time. Since $\frac{100}{ts}=k$ and $\frac{d_{2}}{(2s)(3t)}=k$ through our [[Transitive Property]] we have $\frac{d}{ts}=\frac{d_{2}}{(2s)(3t)}$. Taking $\frac{d}{ts}$ instead of $\frac{100}{ts}$ because we want to see how $d$ changes but we cannot know it's exact value. (We could've done $\frac{100}{ts}$ but what I DID WORKED SO YAY!)
	- Rearranging $\frac{d}{ts}=\frac{d_{2}}{(2s)(3t)}$ gives $\frac{d}{d_{2}}=\frac{1}{6}$. This means that the ratio of $d$ to $d_{2}$ is $1:6$, meaning that $d_{2}$ is $6$ times $d$, so we have $600$ MILES.
- Solution 2, book way
	- We have to figure out how Dales, speed, time, and distance covered is related. If Dale drove for $1$ hour at $50mph$ then he will obviously cover $50mi$. If he drives $50mph$ for $2$ hours we have $50*2=100mi$. Similarly, if he drives $50mph$ for $t$ hours we have $50t$ distance. So distance and time is [[Direct Proportion\|Directly Proportional]].
	- What about the effect of [[Rate]]? If Dale doubles his rate from $50mph$ to $100mph$ He'll drive $100$ miles in an hour. If he quadruples his rate from $50mph*4=200mph$, he drives $200mph$ in an hour. So Dale's distance and his rate are [[Direct Proportion\|Directly Proportional]]. 
	- We can put this together and say that Dale's distance is jointly proportional to his rate and his time. Our examples show that we can make an even stronger statement, [[RT=D Rate times Time equals Distance]]. 
	- He travels $d=rt$, and he has $100=rt$ since he traveled a 100 miles. If he doubles is rate it goes to $200=2rt$, tripling his time gives us $600=(2r)(3t)$. (If Dale Doubles or Triples his rate or time, we multiply the whole equation by it to keep it equal!)
