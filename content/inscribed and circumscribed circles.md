## Inscribed Circles
- You know how to find the [[midpoint]] when you have 2 [[point\|points]], but what if you have 3? 
	- How can you find a centrally located point among the 3? It becomes more difficult
- If you're given 3 points you have at least 3 options for finding a central location
	- Imagine the 3 points as the [[vertex\|vertices]] of a [[Triangle]]
		- The first choice is to find the center of the largest [[Circle]] you can fit inside the [[Triangle]]
		- ! Such a point will be the same [[Perpendicular]]-[[Distance]] from each [[Line Segment\|side]] of the triangle
			- This is called an inscribed circle of the triangle
			- $ An inscribed circle of any [[Polygon]] is the largest circle that can fit inside the polygon
				- [[creating an inscribed circle of a triangle with a compass]]


## Circumscribed Circles
- The second choice is to find the point equidistant from all [[vertex\|vertices]] in the triangle
	- To do so you can construct a circle through the 3 [[vertex\|vertices]] 
	- such a circle is called a circumscribed circle
- The circumscribed circle of a [[Triangle]] is a circle that passes through all [[vertex\|vertices]] of that [[Triangle]]. 
	- The distance between the center of the circle and each of the vertices will be equal because each segment between the [[vertex]] and the center of the circle is a [[radius]]
	- You can create a circumscribed circle, whose center is also equidistant to all points of the triangle but in a different way than inscribed circles
		- [[creating a circumscribed circle of a triangle]]
