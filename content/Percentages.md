- Percentages means "per hundred", it's a [[Ratio]] a shortcut to writing a number to a 100.
	- A [[centimeter]], 1/100 meter, or 1cm:100cm (1 Meter) , it's a part to whole ratio.
- Typically use % symbol to write percent.
- Percent is a [[Fraction]], or a [[Ratio]], or [[decimal]] they all act the same 


## Percent in context
When we see a percent, like 42%, using the fact that it is a part to a whole ratio, the value to 100, 42 per 100 can be represented with [[Fraction]] fully because it is a part to a whole ratio.  42/100, which can be written as a [[decimal]], 0.42. All percent means is divide by 100!

- Solution
	- We let a [[variable math\|variable]] define our number, let x represent the number
		- We are told that $x$ is $60$% of $250$, this means that $60:100$ is equivalent to $x:250$
		- Another way to interpret it is that $\frac{60}{100}*250=x$ because [[of means multiplication]]
	- We have the [[Linear Equation]] $\frac{x}{250}=\frac{60}{100}$
	- We could also just start with $\frac{x}{250}=\frac{60}{100}$ at the start, because we know that $x$ is some fraction of 250, and we know that $\frac{x}{250}$ has to be equal to $\frac{60}{100}$ since
	- Solving gives $x=150$, so $150$ is the number that is 60% of 250

- Another way of thinking
	- We know that [[of means multiplication]] and $60$% is just $\frac{60}{100}$
	- We have $250*0.6=x$ 
- Percent means per hundred, if $a$ is $x$ percent of $b$ we have
	- $\frac{a}{b}=\frac{x}{100}$
	- We can rearrange this to be $a=\frac{x}{100}*b$
	- We can write our percentage as a [[decimal]] for quickly computing the answer
- $62.5$% of $48$ is just $48*0.625=30$

> [!NOTE] Concept:
> Knowing conversions between [[Fraction\|Fractions]] to [[decimal\|Decimals]] can prove useful.
> - If we knew that $0.625=\frac{5}{8}$ then we just have $\frac{48*5}{8}$ which is easy to compute $\frac{240}{8}=30$

- Solution 1 my thinking

	- We write $.2$ as a fraction over $100$ as $\frac{20}{100}$ which is 20%

	- Solving this [[Linear Equation]] gives $\frac{20}{100}$ where $x=20$, so $48$ is $20$% of $240$
	- Meaning that the ratio of $48$:$240$ when written as a ratio to 100 is $20$:$100$

	- We have $36=1.20*x$ because that is literally what the problem states, $36$ *is* or $=$ to $1.20$ $of$, or $*$ by $x$

### 6.18
- Key ideas
	- We cannot just "add/subtract" different percentages to get the value of [[Percent Increase and Decrease]] since the value will change when you percent increase/decrease the first time, then the second time will take that new value
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240514185659.png]]
- Bogus Solution for A
	- If we let $x$ be the price of the avocados, we can add $20+25$% to get $45$% which gives us a $145$% increase.
	- This is wrong because we apply both percentages at the same time, he does first $20$%, then adds $25$% of the **new** value
- (a) REAL SOLUTION
	- Let the price of the avocado be $x$, our first increase gets it to be $1.2x$, then we multiply $1.2x$ by $1.25$ to get $(1.2*1.25)x$ which is $1.5x$ which gives us $50$% from the original
- (b) 
	- BOGUS SOLUTION: We decrease the price by $50$% to get $100$%
	- Once again, we add instead of multiplying, taking $50$% of $1.5x$ gives $0.75x$, our goal is to get $x$ to be $1x$, we do this by subtracting $.5x$, which is $50$% of the ORIGINAL value, which is $\frac{1}{3}$ of the current value, so $33.333$%
	- Another (better and more reliable) way to do it is by seeing what we multiply $1.5$ to get $1$, so $1.5*x=1$ and we get $0.666$ or $\frac{2}{3}$, so we multiply $1.5x$ by $\frac{2}{3}$ to go to the original value, which means decreasing it by $\frac{1}{3}$
### 6.19

![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240514193055.png]]
- Solution 1
	- To find the percentage of students who participate in MATHCOUNTS we must first find the ratio of the participants to non participants, we let $x$ represent the number of $7th$ graders
		- $1.5x+x$ represents the total amount of students, $1.5x$ representing the number of 8th graders
			- We take $6$% of $1.5x$ and $3$% of $x$ to get $0.09x+0.03x=0.12x$ which represents the NUMBER of students who participate in MATHCOUNTS
			- The ratio of this to the total amount of students is $0.12x:2.5x$, which is $\frac{0.12}{2.5}$
			- We have $\frac{0.12}{2.5}=\frac{x}{100}$, and this becomes a simple [[Percent Problems#What percent of x is y?\|What percent of x is y]] problem
		- This gives $x=0.048$ which is $4.8$%, so $4.8$% of 7th and 8th graders participate in MATHCOUNTS
	- Note that we did not figure out the exact number of students who went to the school, or were in 7th or 8th grade, in fact, this is impossible to do
		- We simply let $x$ become the amount of 7th graders, and hoped the $x$ would cancel out
		- The actual percentage will stay the same no matter what the $x$ is
		- Since the value of $x$ does not matter, we can "choose" or [[substitution\|Substitute a value for]] $x$, if we chose $100$, we would still get that $4.8$% of 8th and 7th graders went, a good way of checking our answer
	- This is a new concept called [[Relative Information]]

### 6.20
- Key Ideas
	- We must consider the situation of the problem, use variables for unknown values
![[Files/Images/Pasted image 20240515203214.png]]
- BOGUS SOLUTION
	- $180mL$ of a solution, $20$% of $180$ is $36$, we want to know how many $mL$ of the solution should be replaced to change the $20$% to $30$%
	- We pretend that $30$% of the solution already is acid, and calculate how much of the solution that is
		- $30$% of $180$ is $54$, and $20$% of the solution ($36mL$) is already inside the solution, so we find the difference between the two values to get $18mL$
		- We have to replace $18mL$ of the solution to have a solution that is $30$% acid
	- This is a bogus solution since when we take out Solution to replace, we are also taking some of the acid since it's all one solution!
- Real Solution 1
	- We let $x$ be the amount of solution that must be replaced
	- In the end, we will have 180mL of the solution, and $30$% (or $54$mL will have to be acid solution.)
	- We have to figure out how much of the solution is acid after $x$ of the solution is taken out
		- We know that $20$% of $180$ is $36$, so when we take out $x$ from $180$, we are taking $0.2x$ of the acid out (because $20$% is acid and we are taking $x$ mL out so when we take out $x$mL 20% of the amount we take it will be acid)
	- $20$% of $x$ is how much of $36$mL we take out of the solution when we take out $x$mL
		- So we have $36-0.2x$ as the amount of acid left when we $180-x$ (subtract x from 180)
		- We also know that $(36-0.2x)+x$ is how much acid is left when we add $x$ back since $x$ is $100$% acid solution
	- $30$% of $180$ is 54, so we have $(36-0.2x)+x=54$ 
	- Solving gives $x=22.5$, so $22.5mL$ of the original solution must be replaced with $100$% acid
