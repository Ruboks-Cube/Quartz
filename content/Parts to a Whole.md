# What is parts to a whole?
- Refers to when we have a ratio of a part to a whole, a whole being the total of something, and a part being a part of that whole
- A whole is a finished group, the total, we get the whole by adding all parts of the ratio

# Ratio Problem
I have 70 super smart friends, among those 70 smart friends, there are 11 mathy people for every 3 non mathy people. How many super-smart mathy people do I have?

In this problem we are given the total amount of people, 70.
We also get a part-to-part ratio, we split the people into 2 groups, mathy people and non mathy people.

The problem is asking for a relationship between the total people and the mathy people, so we need a ratio between mathy people and all the people because then we are able to figure out the groupings. In the [[Ratio]] problems, in the [[Ratio]] note, we found how much one value changed so we know what to multiply by to change the other, 5 pencils to 3 markers, how many pencils if 6 markers? 6/3 is 2, meaning we multiply 3 by 2 to get 6. So doing the same to 5, we get 10 pencils. We need to do the same in this problem, just with mathy people and all people, so we have an extra step.
## Intuitive Solution
How do we do this problem?

1. We add to find how many people there are in a group. 11 math people for every 3 non math people means that in a group of 14 people, 11 of them are mathy and 3 are not in a group of 14 people. The ratio between mathy people and all people is 11:14, for every 14 people, 11 of them are mathy. <mark class="hltr-blue">Notice how it's not 14 + 11 here like the part to part ratio, it's actually 11/14, since in 11 out of 14 people are mathy.</mark>

$$11+3=14$$
2. We divide 70 (the total number of people) by the people per group to find how many groups there are
$$\frac{70}{14}=5$$

3. Now that we know we have 5 groups for the ratio 11:3 we multiply 11 (the mathy people) by 5, to find how many math people there are in 70 people where there are 5 groups of 14.
$$5\cdot11=55$$
And there you have it, there are 55 mathy people.

### other Key takeaways

This is a problem that helps us utilize [[whole of a group]], it is basically just parts and wholes. Like a [[Fraction]].

Think about it, in this problem, we have 11 mathy people per 3 non math people, that represents a group of 14 people, we can think of the 14 as a whole and the 11 and 3 as the parts. 

We can represent ratios as fractions now. 11:3 also means 11/14 and 3/14, 14 being the total and 11 and 3 being the parts.
