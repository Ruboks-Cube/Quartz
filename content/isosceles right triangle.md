#math/geometry/2d/shape/Triangle 


An [[isosceles triangle]] that is also a [[Right Triangle]] is a triangle that has both qualities. The only way for this to happen is if the 2 legs of the [[Right Triangle]] are equal.

So what is special about these [[Triangle\|Triangles]]?
### Hypotenuse Rule
Lets take a look at one:
![[Files/Images/isosceles right triangle 2024-02-26 18.59.31.excalidraw]]
We need to find $y$ of $\triangle ABC$, we do this with the [[Pythagorean Theorem]].
- $\overline {AB}$ and $\overline{BC}$ are both $x$, so this is an [[isosceles triangle]]!
- find $y$ by doing $x^2+x^2=y^2$  
- $y=\sqrt{x^2+x^2 }$
- $y=\sqrt{ 2x^2 }$
- $y=x\sqrt{ 2 }$ due to the [[separating and combing radical expressions]] rule

Conclusion: For a isosceles right triangle the hypotenuse is always going to be the length of the leg times the [[Square root of 2]]

### Angle Rule
What is angle $p?$
![[Files/Images/isosceles right triangle 2024-02-26 19.22.37.excalidraw]]
All we are given in this problem is that this is an [[isosceles triangle]] with a [[Right Angle]].
We want to find angle p, and intuitively, we can know that $\angle p$ is $45^{\circ}$ because 2 sides are equal so the angle must also be equal. And this makes sense because the shape is symmetrical. 

2 angles are 45, 1 angle is 90, for this reason isosceles right triangles are known as 45-45-90 triangles.

### Practice Problems
Find area of $\triangle STU$
![[Files/Images/isosceles right triangle 2024-02-26 19.09.39.excalidraw]]
Due to the rule, we know that $10=\overline{ST}(\sqrt{ 2 })$
(Length of $\overline{ST}$ times $\sqrt{ 2 }$)

Dividing the equation by $\sqrt{ 2 }$ gives us $\frac{10}{\sqrt{ 2 }}=\overline{ST}$

We now know the side lengths, lets get the area
$\frac{1}{2}*{\frac{10}{\sqrt{ 2 }}}*{\frac{10}{\sqrt{ 2 }}}$ 
This is the same as
$\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{10*10}{\sqrt{ 2 }*\sqrt{ 2 }} \right)$
Which is
$\frac{1}{2}\left( \frac{100}{2} \right)$
Which simplifies to
$25$
